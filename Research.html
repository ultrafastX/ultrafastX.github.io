Generation and investigation of ultrafast phenomena with X-ray free-electron lasers

X-ray free-electron lasers (XFELs) deliver intense light pulses with femtosecond temporal resolution and hundreds of nanometers spatial resolution, thus making them an ideal tool to probe the molecular-scale structure of matter on timescales that are characteristic to molecular motion—or, in other words, to record "movies" of atoms and molecules moving and reorganizing into different structures.

XFELs are a new science tool, and to realize their potential of seeing the matter "in action", we need to discover how to conduct experiments on important problems and to interpret the data, while keeping an eye open to new phenomena that might occur. Equally important, XFELs can also help us understand phenomena that occur at the micro- and nanoscale, but occur too rapidly to be investigated experimentally.

In this area of research, my interests reside at the intersection of XFELs with hydrodynamics, metastable systems, and phase transitions. To conduct such experiments, we developed ultrafast imaging techniques that use both optical and X-ray lasers. A major part of my recent work focused on hydrodynamic phenomena induced by X-ray lasers in liquids. I am also working on studies of rapid ice crystallization in water, of the chemical dynamics during the photosynthetic cycle in nature, and of the dynamics of biomolecules under the extreme conditions generated by XFEL irradiation.

Liquid microexplosions induced by XFELs

X-ray pulses produced at XFEL facilities are so intense that a single one is sufficient to destroy the sample under investigation. Often, XFEL experiments must be performed serially on a large number of identical samples, with a new sample for each X-ray pulse. The most frequently used technique for enabling such serial experiments is embedding the samples in continuously flowing liquid jets. The jets are also subject to X-ray damage, which may impede efforts to run experiments at high repetition rates.

We used ultrafast optical imaging to determine the dynamics of the explosions induced by XFEL pulses in liquid microjets. Our initial goal was to determine if, and how, microexplosions induced by X-rays affect experiments. We found that the effects of explosions can still be seen at time delays a billion times longer than the duration of the pulse, and affect the jets over distances up to a hundred times larger than the diameter of the X-ray beam. These microexplosions are multistep processes in which rapid high-energy phenomena drive slower dynamics. For example, picosecond isochoric heating by X-rays drives nanosecond shock waves, which in turn lead to spectacular microsecond hydrodynamics.

Generation of deeply-metastable stretched water with XFELs

If a liquid with free interfaces is at a pressure below its vapor pressure, it is thermodynamically unstable and will either evaporate or boil. Nevertheless, it is possible to generate metastable liquids at pressures below the vapor pressure, or even in stretched states (corresponding to a negative pressure). Liquid water can be subjected to negative pressures for extended periods of time, but will cavitate if stretched too far. Using a few special techniques it is possible produce metastable water at pressures down to about -200 to −300 atmospheres, but these pressures are far from the pressure expected for homogenous cavitation (around -1500 atmospheres).

Using the reflection of shock waves produced by XFEL pulses at the surface of drops of water, we stretched water until it ruptured through spallation, on a few-nanosecond time scale. We determined from the spallation dynamics that pressures below -1000 atmospheres were briefly reached in the liquid—deeper than in all previous experiments that used unconfined water. We proposed a heterogeneous cavitation model that predicts that in order to break the apparent “negative pressure barrier” of -300 atmospheres, water must be stretched in a few nanoseconds or less. While such stretching rates may appear impractically rapid, we showed that they can be acheived, and the extreme conditions to which we subject liquid water could help us understand its unusual thermdynamic properties.

Metastable and cubic ice

Normal water ice, such as the ice we make in our freezers, is a thermodynamically stable solid with a crystal structure that has a hexagonal molecular symmetry ("hexagonal ice"), which manifests itself in the familiar hexagonal shape of showflakes. But with a slight change in how the molecules are arranged in ice, crystals with cubic crystal symmetry ("cubic ice") may form. Cubic ice can form when supercooled water freezes at temperatures below 0 °C, but this ice is metastable and will transform into hexagonal ice. Observation of haloes around the sun suggest that crystals of pure cubic ice may form in the atmosphere, and if this is the case, its difference in vapor pressure relative to normal ice would affect how rain and snow form in the clouds. Scientists have attempted to make cubic ice in the lab by freezing supercooled water, but so far the ice produced is not purely cubic - which raises the questions of how cubic one can make the ice, and whether pure cubic ice can actually exist.

A good strategy for observing highy cubic ice is to supercool as much as possible liquid water before freezing, and then probe the ice that forms as soon as possible - to avoid its transformaton to normal hexagonal ice. Our collaborators - Barbara Wyslouzil's group from Ohio State - can produce nanosized water droplets in a supersonic nozzle, and then supercool these nanodrops to below -50 °C before freezing. We have used the X-ray laser at SLAC to determine, less than 100 microseconds after freezing, what type of ice forms during this extreme case of freezing. The analysis of the X-ray diffraction from nanodrops indicates that our ice was 78% cubic, more than other ices produced in the lab, and also more than theoretically predicted limits for how cubic ice can be.

The investigation of cubic ice is important not only for a better understanding of atmospheric processes, but also as an example of rapid nonequilibrum solidification leading to the formation of a metastable solid. When a liquid freezes rapidly, the molecules in the liquid do not have to arrange themselves a perfectly ordered crystal, therefore the formation of metastable or imperfect crystals is not suprising. However, little is known about how an individual molecule, or a group of a few molecules, actually build up the crystal. The combination of rapid freezing with ultrafast diffraction from X-ray lasers offers a promising route to understand or even "see" how rapid nonequilibrium solidification occurs at a molecular scale.

The nucleation of ice in supercooled water

Pure water in micron-sized drops can be supercooled to temperatures close to -40 ºC, where it spontaneously freezes due to homogeneous nucleation. Such supercooled drops have been observed in the atmosphere, but reliable production of supercooled water at such temperatures in the lab remains challenging, and I worked towards better methods for producing, handling, and investigating supercooled water and the nucleation of ice.

We developed a high-accuracy and high-speed instrument for studying supercooled water by investigating how statistical ensembles (more than 10 000 systems) of ~100-micron diameter drops of water freeze by homogenous nucleation of ice. With this instrument, we were able to measure the rate of homogeneous nucleation of ice with low noise and excellent reproducibility. This experimental platform is faster and more reliable than any other previous instruments for the study of ice nucleation, and it also allows to image at high resolution the freezing dynamics, such as the growth of ice crystals inside drops of supercooled water.

In another ice nucleation project we investigated whether external electric fields have an effect on the homogenous nucleation of ice in bulk water. We applied larger electric fields than in any previous experiments, and we have not observed any changes in the rates of nucleation.

The most promising application of this experimental platform, however, is the investigation of heterogeneous nucleation of ice on foreign particles present in water; the high data throughput of the ice nucleation instrument allows us to measure quickly the probability of heterogeneous nucleation over a wide range of temperatures—a feat that is difficult to achieve using other instruments.

The hydrodynamics of drop generation and transport in microchannels

I also studied the production and physical properties of two-phase, liquid-liquid microfluidic systems such as (i) laminar flow structures which we used as opto-fluidic components, and (ii) drops of liquid inside another liquid phase which can be used as micro-enclosures for chemical reactions. We showed that variation of fluid properties through temperature control extends the degree of control and the range of operation of microfluidic drop and bubble generators. In another series of projects, we investigated the transverse hydrodynamic forces (lift forces) on drops and bubbles and microchannels; we discovered that under microfluidic conditions these forces are stronger than at larger scales, thus making them particularly effective for applications that require the transverse positioning of drops and bubbles.
